## What is actually in the can?

Baking powder combines three jobs in one dry mixture:

- **A base:** usually sodium bicarbonate—the same chemical used as baking soda.
- **One or more dry acids:** these react with the base after moisture reaches them.
- **A starch:** this helps keep the acid and base dry and separated in storage.

That built-in acid is the important difference between baking powder and baking soda. Baking soda needs enough acid elsewhere in the recipe. Baking powder brings its own.

## What “double acting” means

Most grocery-store baking powder is double acting. It contains acids that react at different times: some gas is released after mixing, and more is released as the food heats. Utah State University Extension describes this as one rise immediately and another when heated.

That second reaction gives you a little breathing room, but not unlimited time. Once a wet batter is mixed, get it into the prepared pan and oven instead of letting it conduct a long kitchen meeting.

## Quick freshness test

Penn State Extension recommends this simple check:

1. Put **1 teaspoon baking powder** in a small bowl or cup.
2. Add **⅓ cup hot water**.
3. Look for vigorous foam.

If it barely reacts, replace it. This test tells you whether the powder still produces gas; it does not rescue a recipe with the wrong ratios.

## Baking powder vs. baking soda

| | Baking powder | Baking soda |
|---|---|---|
| Contains a base | Yes | Yes |
| Contains its own acid | Yes | No |
| Needs acid in the recipe | Not for the basic reaction | Yes |
| Can you swap them 1:1? | No | No |

Utah State University Extension lists workable baking-powder substitutions that combine baking soda with an acid such as cream of tartar, but substitutions can change flavor, texture, or volume. When the recipe matters, matching the intended leavener is the cheap safe move.
