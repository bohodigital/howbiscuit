  A better answer does four things: it answers the question early, explains why the answer is true, makes the next step usable, and shows where its evidence or confidence runs out.

Length is not the test. A six-word answer can be useless. A long answer can be excellent. The question is whether each part earns the reader’s attention.

## The four-part test

<MechanismSteps steps={[
  { title: 'Does it answer the real question?', body: 'A useful response identifies what the reader is actually trying to decide, understand, or fix—and says the answer before wandering into background.' },
  { title: 'Can you follow the reason?', body: 'Rules become reusable when the mechanism, logic, or evidence is visible. “Because I said so” is not a portable skill.' },
  { title: 'Is the next move obvious?', body: 'Examples, steps, checks, and stop conditions turn information into something a reader can use.' },
  { title: 'Can you see the limits?', body: 'Strong answers separate facts from judgment, link important sources, and admit uncertainty instead of decorating it with confidence.' }
]} />

## Compare two answers

**Question:** Why did my baking powder stop working?

<BiscuitBox variant="common-mistake" title="Weak answer">
  It probably expired. Buy a new one.
</BiscuitBox>

The weak answer might be right, but it gives the reader no check and no useful understanding.

<BiscuitBox variant="short-answer" title="Better answer">
  Moisture and time can reduce baking powder’s activity. Test one teaspoon in one-third cup of hot water: vigorous foam means it still has rising power. If the reaction is weak, replace the can. Store the next one dry and covered.
</BiscuitBox>

The second answer names the likely cause, gives a safe check, defines what the result means, and provides a next move. It also creates a claim that can be checked against a real source.

## Traceability builds trust

When an answer depends on facts, the reader should be able to tell where those facts came from. That does not mean dumping a bibliography onto every ordinary sentence. It means linking the claims that matter and preferring sources close to the evidence: official guidance, standards, textbooks, universities, peer-reviewed research, and direct documentation.

Purdue Online Writing Lab’s guidance on paragraph development emphasizes specific evidence plus analysis—not evidence dropped on the page and left to fend for itself. A source should support the reasoning; it should not replace it.

## A useful answer is a small teaching system

The best practical answers do more than close one question. They leave the reader better equipped for the next version of the problem. That is why How Biscuit pages use worked examples, common mistakes, source notes, evidence labels, cheap safe alternatives, and related guides.

<BiscuitBox variant="source-note" title="Our editorial rule">
  If an answer cannot show its reasoning, evidence, or limits, it is not ready to borrow the reader’s confidence.
</BiscuitBox>
