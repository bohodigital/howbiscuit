# How Biscuit Constitution v0.2

Date: 2026-07-01
Status: strategy and editorial constitution draft
Scope: How Biscuit only

## 1. Core Identity

How Biscuit is a broad practical explainer site for people trying to understand, fix, cook, calculate, research, buy, or make do without wading through content sludge.

The public promise:

> Plain answers for school, cooking, home tech, and making do.

The internal promise:

> Explain the mechanism, show the practical move, name the failure modes, and give the cheap safe version.

How Biscuit is not a content farm, fake review site, homework cheating service, affiliate landfill, AI article dump, trend mill, fake expertise costume, or monetization-first publication.

## 2. Market Reality

The broad explainer market already has old, deep, trusted competitors. A new broad site cannot win by being broad. It must win by having a sharper method.

Observed competitor patterns:

- Broad explainer sites win by covering many categories with stable navigational systems.
- Math sites win by having full course maps, worked examples, study aids, and common-error support.
- Cooking education sites win through science, testing, practical guides, and ingredient/equipment literacy.
- Home tech pages are split between official safety guidance, manufacturer docs, forums, and gear-review sites.
- Cheap-living content is noisy and often unsafe, which leaves room for practical, low-cost, safety-bounded guides.
- AI writing guidance is high-demand but ethically risky, so it needs disclosure, source verification, and academic-integrity boundaries.

Therefore, How Biscuit should not behave like a general blog. It should behave like a practical knowledge base with several disciplined divisions.

## 3. Strategic Positioning

How Biscuit's edge is the combination of:

1. answer-first explanations
2. practical steps
3. mechanism-level clarity
4. common mistakes
5. source notes
6. cheap safe alternatives
7. calculators, checklists, templates, and examples
8. honest evidence labels
9. internal links that actually help readers continue learning

The site should be funny and blunt where appropriate, but clarity always beats cleverness. A joke may season the page; it may not replace the answer.

## 4. Current Mission

The current mission is not monetization.

The current mission is:

1. stabilize the static site
2. build a clean publishing workflow
3. create reader-facing navigation
4. publish coherent clusters, not random posts
5. get pages crawled and indexed
6. connect Search Console and analytics
7. learn which formats produce impressions, clicks, and useful engagement
8. build trust before affiliate links, ads, sponsorships, or lead capture

No monetization strategy may drive early editorial decisions.

## 7. Major Division: Research & Writing

Path: `/research-writing/`

Mission: help students and independent learners research, cite, outline, revise, and use AI honestly.

This section may cover:

```text

/research-writing/citations/
/research-writing/literature-reviews/
/research-writing/essay-structure/
/research-writing/ai-writing/
/research-writing/notes-and-outlines/
/research-writing/avoiding-plagiarism/
/research-writing/research-tools/
```

Required stance:

- AI can help with brainstorming, outlining, feedback, source discovery, summarization checks, and revision support.
- AI may not be treated as a verified source.
- AI-generated citations must be checked against real sources.
- Assignment rules and instructor policies matter.
- Disclosure guidance must be practical and style-aware.

Allowed page types:

- how to cite AI in MLA or APA
- how to verify a source exists
- how to turn notes into an outline
- how to use AI to ask better research questions
- how to revise a paragraph without losing your own voice
- how to avoid accidental plagiarism

Banned page types:

- write-my-essay pages
- assignment evasion guides
- fake citation generators
- policies that encourage hiding AI use
- content that treats hallucinated sources as acceptable

The editorial tone may be blunt: the reader should leave knowing that AI is useful, but that handing it the steering wheel and napping in the back seat is how academic disasters are lovingly manufactured.

## 16. Source Standard

Every factual page needs source notes.

Preferred source order:

1. official primary sources
2. standards bodies and government guidance
3. peer-reviewed or textbook-level sources
4. university and extension sources
5. reputable expert publications
6. carefully labeled firsthand testing or observation
7. user anecdotes only as leads, never as proof

Source preferences by division:

- Math: open textbooks, university notes, well-established texts, worked verification.
- Research & Writing: MLA, APA, Purdue OWL, university library guidance, assignment policies when applicable.
- Cooking: USDA/FDA food safety, university extension, food-science references, reputable tested cooking publications.
- Home Tech: FTC, CISA/NIST where applicable, manufacturer documentation, standards, reputable testing.
- Make-Do Lab: official safety guidance, DOE/CDC/extension sources, manuals, lease-safe common-sense constraints.
- Buying Guides: manufacturer specs, manuals, official compatibility docs, tested reviews, and declared evidence level.

A page may cite competitors when they are the best available public explanation, but it must add value beyond paraphrase.

## 17. AI Use Policy

AI may be used for research planning, outlines, drafts, examples, QA checklists, source discovery, and rewrite support.

AI may not be used as a final authority.

Before publication:

- factual claims must be checked against real sources
- citations must be opened and verified
- math must be solved independently or checked computationally
- code/tools must be tested
- product claims must be evidence-labeled
- no fake firsthand experience may be added

If AI meaningfully shapes a published page, the internal draft record should say so.

## 18. Academic Integrity Policy

How Biscuit teaches. It does not do cheating.

Allowed:

- explain problem types
- show original worked examples
- teach methods
- build practice problems
- explain citation and source use
- help readers understand feedback

Banned:

- custom answers to active graded assignments presented as submission-ready work
- essay-writing-for-submission pages
- hiding AI use when disclosure is required
- fake citations
- plagiarism workflows

## 19. Safety and High-Stakes Content

How Biscuit may explain high-stakes topics only as general education and with clear limits.

Restricted areas:

- medical advice
- legal advice
- financial advice
- electrical repairs
- structural repairs
- tenant/legal rights
- cybersecurity beyond consumer basics
- food safety claims
- heat illness and extreme weather safety

For these areas, pages must use stronger sources, clearer disclaimers, and conservative recommendations.

## 20. Technical Constitution

The site must remain fast, crawlable, static-first, and easy to validate.

Required:

- public pages return HTTP 200
- each public page has one canonical URL
- each public page has a unique title
- each public page has a unique meta description
- sitemap is accurate
- RSS is accurate for new substantial articles
- robots.txt points to sitemap
- important content works without client-side JavaScript
- CSS and assets are crawlable
- mobile layout is readable
- no broken internal links
- no placeholder production links
- dates are honest
- source notes are present before publish

Preferred future workflow:

```text
content/articles/*.md
content/tools/*.md
content/glossary/*.md
templates/base.html
templates/article.html
templates/hub.html
data/navigation.json
data/site.json
scripts/build-site.py
public/
```

The generator should refuse to publish drafts, missing metadata, missing source notes, empty pages, and placeholder text.

## 21. SEO Doctrine

SEO is allowed. Search-engine-first sludge is not.

Allowed:

- descriptive titles
- useful headings
- coherent hubs
- internal links
- external citations
- schema when accurate
- fast pages
- accessible images
- answer-first structure
- tools and checklists

Banned:

- keyword stuffing
- fake freshness
- fake authors
- fake expertise
- fake reviews
- scraped summaries
- scaled low-value pages
- doorway pages
- programmatic junk
- unsupported best claims
- changing five variables and pretending the result means something

The early goal is not revenue. The early goal is crawlability, indexing, data, and proof that readers and search systems respond to the site's useful structure.

## 22. Experiment Rules

Every meaningful SEO/content experiment must be logged before it starts.

Required fields:

- experiment ID
- affected URL or cluster
- hypothesis
- variable changed
- date started
- baseline metrics
- success metric
- expected result
- confounders
- result
- decision
- rollback path

Do not change five things at once unless the experiment is explicitly labeled as a broad redesign rather than a controlled test.

## 23. Launch Phases

### Phase 0: Shell

Done when:

- homepage is reader-facing
- top navigation exists
- footer trust links exist
- trust pages exist
- sitemap/RSS/robots are correct
- no public placeholders exist

### Phase 1: First Clusters

Done when:

- Math, Make-Do Lab, Home Tech, Cooking, and Tools have live hubs
- at least 15 substantial pages are live
- all pages are internally linked
- Search Console and analytics are connected
- first monthly measurement report exists

### Phase 2: Foundation

Done when:

- at least 30 substantial pages are live
- six major hubs exist
- glossary has only cluster-supported entries
- at least 5 experiments are logged
- top and weak pages are identified from real data

### Phase 3: Scale

Done when:

- publishing workflow is automated or checklist-enforced
- 45 to 60 strong pages exist
- tools section has multiple useful utilities
- content updates are scheduled
- monetization-readiness review is complete

Monetization may be considered only after Phase 2 and should normally wait until Phase 3.

## 26. Governing Rule

How Biscuit exists to be useful enough that the reader does not immediately need to search again.

When deciding what to publish, ask:

1. Does this answer a real reader question?
2. Does it belong to a cluster?
3. Does it add something beyond search-result paraphrase?
4. Is it sourced or clearly reasoned?
5. Is it safe?
6. Is it honest about evidence?
7. Does it link to the next useful page?
8. Would this still be worth publishing if it earned no money this year?

If the answer is no, do not publish it.
